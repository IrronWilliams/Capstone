Voting in America: Historical Perspective

Date Event
1776 Voting is controlled by individual state legislatures. Only white men age 21 and older who own land can vote.
1848 Abolitionlists and pro women's suffrage groups first meet and organize in Senaca Falls.
1868 The 14th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution grants full citizenship rights, including voting rights, to all men born or naturalized in the United States. .
1870 The 15th Amendment eliminates racial barriers to voting. Many states continue practicing voter discrimination. Poll taxes, literacy tests, fraud, and intimidation still prevent many from voting. Native Americans are still denied the right to vote.
1872 Activists Susan B. Anthony and Soujourner Truth are arrested for trying to vote.
1890 Wyoming becomes the first US state to grant women the right to vote.
1920 The 19th Amendment grants the women the right to vote nationwide. But not all Native American and Asian women have citzenship.
1924 Indian Citzenship Act is passed, giving Native Americans full citzenship. However many states disenfranchise them at the polls.
1952 McCarren-Walter Act is passed, grants all Asian Americans the right to become citizens and vote.
1961 23rd Amendment gives DC residents the right to vote for presidents, but not Congressional representation.
1965 Voting Rights Act is passed, removing discrimanatory barriers that kept many people of color from voting.
1971 Voting age is lowered from 21 to 18 in light of the Vietnam War; to ensure those old enouph to fight could vote.
1975 The federal Voting Rights Act is renewed, permanently banning literacy tests nationwide. Section 203 is added, requiring translated voting materials in areas with large numbers of citizens with limited English skills.
1984 The federal Voting Accessibility for the Elderly and Handicapped Act requires polling places to be accessible to people with disabilities.
1993 National Voter Registration Act is passed, making it easier to register at DMV's and public assistance centers.
2000 A federal court ruled that citizens of US territories like Puerto Rico and Guam CANNOT vote in federal elections.
2009 Washington becomes the second state in the U.S. (after Oregon) to vote entirely by mail rather than in person at the polls.
2018 Amendment 4 is passed, restoring voting rights to over 1.4 million Floridians with past felony convictions. However, efforts to block implementation of new law is under way. Opposing forces argue that payment of court fines and fees should be paid before people with felony records can regain the right to vote.

US Voter Participation Rates by State (2018 Midterm Election)

Data source: US Census Bureau.

The November 2018 election resulted in a historic 11 percentage point increase compared to the 2014 midterm election. The 11% increase was driven by stronger voter participation rates among all voting age and major racial and ethnic groups. 53% of the voting-age population voted in 2018, making the year the highest midterm turnout in four decades. The historic turnout reversed the trajectory of the 2014 election, which had the lowest engagement rate at 42%.

Voting Participation By Gender

In the 2018 midterm election, both men and women voted at historic rates; with double digit turnout increases (11 and 12 percentage points respectively). 55% of eligible female voters cast their ballots in the 2018 midterm election compared to 51.8% of men. As seen in the past five midterm elections, women turned out to vote at slightly higher rates then men.

There were wide differences in voting preferences between men and women. The gender gap in voting preference is not new, but in 2018, the 23-point gap was widest it has been since 1992. Male voters preferred the Republican candidate by 4 percentage points, while female voters preferred their Democratic candidate by 19 points.




Voting Participation By Age

During the 2018 mid-term election, the three younger generations (ages 18-53) casted 62 million votes. 60.1 million votes came from Baby Boomers and older generations. With double digit increases, voter turnout was higher among adults of all ages. Younger adults showed the highest voter turnout increase. Turnout among adults under 25 nearly doubled to 32.4%. Turnout rates for adults 25-34 increased 14 percentage points to 42.1% and 13 points among those between 35-44. Young voters (age 18-29) voted for Democrats by a 35-point margin in 2018. 67% of voters age 18-29 and 58% of voters age 30-44 favored their Democratic candidate. Voters 45 and older were divided among political parties. 50% of this population voted Republican and 49% Democrat.

Although older age groups continued to be more likely than younger groups to vote in the midterms, their turnout increases were more moderate. 66.1% of adults 65 and older voted in the 2018 midterm, up from 59.4% in the 2014 midterm. Those ages 55 to 64 increased their turnout rate by 7.8 percentage points. Baby Boomers, those ages 54 to 72 in 2018, had their highest-ever midterm election turnout (64%, the same rate as the Silent Generation) and cast more votes than they ever have in a midterm (44.1 million).

The difference in turnout by gender varied by age. Woman had a stronger turnout rate across all age groups. Among younger voters (18 to 44), the gender gap was wider than the gap for older voters (45 and older). The gender gap is a follows in percentage points:

Voting Participation By Race

Considering the variable of race provides additional insights; especially around race and educational lines. More white woman voted for Democrats in 2018 than in 2016. In the 2018 mid-term election, their vote were split evenly between Democrats and Republicans (49% to 49%). This shift is notable because in the 2014 mid-term election, white woman votes skewed towards Republicans by 12 points. In the 2018 mid-term, a large number of nonwhite women voted for Democrats. In particular, Hispanic women shifted to the left in greater numbers in 2018.

Education level is a strong predictor in determining a voter's party preferences. White woman voters were divided by their educational backgrounds (a significant 34 point gap). White woman without a college degree voted for Republicans by 14 points (56% to 42%). White women with a college degree voted for Democrats by 20 points (59% to 39%).

In line with the 2016 presidential election, white men favored Republicans and voted Republican 60% to 39% Democrat. African Americans voted overwhelmingly 90% Democrat. Voting by gender within the African American community were comparable. 88% of African American men voted for their Democratic candidate while African American women voted at 92%.
